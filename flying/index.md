# Pilot Logbook Analysis

**393 flights. 540.1 hours. July 2009 to February 2026.**

## The Arc of a Pilot

This logbook tells a story in three acts.

**Act I: The Student (2009-2011).** You started at Chester (SNC) on July 14, 2009, in a C-152 with Matt Izzo. Dutch rolls, slow flight, the whole syllabus grind. You soloed on September 21, 2009 -- just over two months later -- flying from SNC to Tweed-New Haven and back. Fast progression. You passed your Private Pilot checkride on December 2, 2009, less than five months from your first lesson, with about 51 hours. That's efficient.

What followed was a year of doing what newly-minted private pilots should do: taking friends flying. Kate to Fisher's Island and Cape Cod. Seth and Morgan. John and Alana to Montauk. Your mother ("bumpy!"). This is the joy-of-flying phase, and the logbook radiates it.

**Act II: The Gap and the Return (2012-2018).** Then life happened. 2012 had three flights. 2013 had nine. 2014 had four. And then -- a 929-day gap from August 2014 to July 2017. Two and a half years without flying. When you came back, it was at Bridgeport with refresher training, pattern work, the slow process of rebuilding proficiency. 2017 and 2018 were rebuilding years: 18 flights, 20.9 hours combined. You were shaking the rust off.

**Act III: The Instrument Rating and Beyond (2019-present).** 2019 was the inflection point. You went all-in on instrument training with Alan Niemiec (36 flights, 54.9 hours together -- your most significant instructor relationship by far). The logbook from this period reads like an IFR approach plate collection: ILS 2 HVN, VOR 36 MMK, RNAV 29 BDR, holds at STANE, MILUM, MAD, ACELA. Partial panel. Unusual attitudes. Night work. You passed your Instrument checkride on January 23, 2020. Congratulations was earned -- that's a serious rating and you put in the work.

Since then, the trajectory has been steadily upward. Every year more flights, more hours, more ambition:

## Patterns and Curiosities

**Home base migration.** Chester (SNC) to Bridgeport (BDR/KBDR) to Tweed-New Haven (HVN). HVN became home when you bought 4497R, and it's been the center of your flying universe since.

**The autopilot saga.** An unusual number of flights are dedicated to autopilot tuning and testing. This suggests either a particularly finicky installation or (more likely) a pilot who wants their automation *exactly* right before trusting it in IMC.

**VOR 36 MMK as your home approach.** Meriden-Markham is your practice approach playground. It appears dozens of times -- VOR 36 MMK, RNAV 36 MMK -- with holds at MAD and ACELA. This is your backyard instrument proficiency loop.

**Seasonal rhythm.** You fly most in summer and early fall, less in deep winter. But you push into the cold when motivated -- January IPC sessions, December cross-countries. The Feb 28, 2026 entry ("Finally warmed up") after a 48-day winter gap is relatable.

**John as safety pilot.** After getting your instrument rating, John becomes your regular safety pilot for proficiency approaches -- at least a dozen sessions of practice approaches with John in the right seat. A mutual relationship: you were also safety pilot for John ("Safety pilot for John. Headings, altitudes, VOR & GPS nav"). Pilots who fly together stay sharp together.

**"Almost had a lesson" entries.** Two of them -- weather cancellations that you logged anyway. The discipline to show up even when the weather says no.

**The 2021 avionics journey.** Dropped off at Sky Manor in April, picked up in September, then back to Sky Manor in October. Five months of shop time for avionics work. The patience of an aircraft owner.

## About This Report

This analysis was generated by feeding an Excel pilot logbook export into Claude (Anthropic's Opus 4.6 model) and asking it to look for patterns, interesting flights, and the story behind the numbers rather than produce a statistical summary. The logbook data was parsed programmatically; the narrative was written entirely by the model from what it found in the remarks, routes, dates, and flight times. No edits were made to the analysis beyond minor factual corrections.

The prompt was simple: *"Perform a qualitative analysis. I'm not interested so much in numbers as in patterns, interesting flights, anything that you think is worth pointing out."*
